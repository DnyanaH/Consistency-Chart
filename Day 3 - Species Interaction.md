## Species Interactions

Before any definition, here's a story:

> You're watching a lion chase a deer. The deer escapes and eats grass. A tick is feeding on the deer's blood. A bird sits on the deer's back eating those ticks. Nearby, two lions are fighting over the same prey.
> 
> **In that one scene, you just witnessed four different types of species interactions — predation, herbivory, parasitism, mutualism, and competition. Every relationship in nature falls into a category based on who benefits and who is harmed.**

---

### The Master Framework — Learn This First

Every interaction between two species affects each one as **+** (benefits), **–** (harmed), or **0** (unaffected). All interactions are classified by this:
![[Pasted image 20260602140759.png]]
**Memory trick — "MCP PAC"** Mutualism · Commensalism · Predation · Parasitism · Amensalism · Competition

Now let's go deep into each one.

### 1. Mutualism (+/+) — Both Benefit

Both species interact and **both gain** from the relationship. It is obligatory in some cases (neither can survive without the other) and facultative in others (both benefit but can survive alone).

**Most important examples for NET:**

|Example|Species 1|Species 2|What each gets|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Rhizobium + legume**|Rhizobium bacteria|Bean/pea plant|Bacteria fix N₂ → plant gets nitrogen; bacteria get shelter + food|
|**Mycorrhizae + plant roots**|Fungi|Plant|Fungi get sugar; plant gets water + minerals (phosphorus)|
|**Bee + flower**|Bee|Flower|Bee gets nectar; flower gets pollinated|
|**Lichen**|Algae|Fungi|Algae photosynthesize (food); fungi provide structure + water|
|**Clownfish + sea anemone**|Clownfish|Sea anemone|Fish gets protection; anemone gets cleaning + luring of prey|
|**Cleaner wrasse + large fish**|Wrasse (small fish)|Large fish|Wrasse eats parasites (food); large fish gets cleaned|

**Exam alert:** Lichen is the classic example of mutualism AND the pioneer species in primary succession — connecting two topics. Mycorrhizae are extremely high frequency in NET papers.

**Obligate vs Facultative mutualism:**

- **Obligate** — neither can survive without the other (e.g. lichen — algae and fungi cannot live separately in harsh environments)
- **Facultative** — both benefit but can survive alone (e.g. bee and flower)

---

### 2. Predation (+/–) — Predator & Prey

The predator **kills and consumes** the prey. The predator benefits (+), the prey is harmed (–, i.e. dies).

**Why predation is ecologically important — NET loves this:**

- Controls prey population (natural population regulation)
- Drives **natural selection** — only fast/strong prey survive → evolution
- Maintains **species diversity** — keystone predators prevent one species from dominating

**Keystone species concept** — a predator whose removal causes dramatic ecosystem collapse disproportionate to its numbers.

> Classic example: **Sea otters** eat sea urchins. Remove otters → urchins explode → urchins eat all kelp → kelp forest collapses → entire ecosystem collapses. Sea otters are a keystone species.

**Lotka-Volterra Model** — predator-prey population cycles. As prey increases → predators increase → prey decreases → predators decrease → prey recovers. This creates a wave-like oscillation. Directly asked in NET.

**Herbivory** — a special type of predation where the "predator" is an animal eating a plant. Plant is harmed but rarely killed entirely (unlike true predation).

**Plant defences against herbivory** — another NET favourite:

- **Physical:** thorns (rose), spines (cactus), thick bark
- **Chemical:** tannins, alkaloids (caffeine, nicotine, morphine), latex (rubber plant)
- **Mimicry:** some plants mimic inedible plants

---

### 3. Competition (–/–) — Both Lose

Both species are harmed because they compete for the **same limited resource** — food, space, light, water, mates.

**Two types of competition:**
![[Pasted image 20260602140927.png]]
**The Competitive Exclusion Principle (Gause's Law)** — one of the most tested concepts in NET:

> **Two species competing for the identical ecological niche cannot coexist indefinitely. One will out-compete and eliminate the other.**

Proposed by **G.F. Gause (1934)** — also called **Gause's Competitive Exclusion Principle.**

**BUT** — in nature, species coexist by **niche differentiation** — they slightly shift their resource use to avoid direct competition. This is called **resource partitioning.**

> Example: Five warbler species live in the same spruce tree in North America, but each feeds at a _different part_ of the tree. They avoid competing directly by partitioning the habitat.

---

### 4. Parasitism (+/–) — Parasite & Host

The **parasite** benefits; the **host** is harmed but **not killed immediately** (unlike predation — this is the key difference).

**Two types:**

|Type|Meaning|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|**Ectoparasite**|Lives _outside_ the host body|Ticks, lice, fleas, mosquitoes, Cuscuta (plant)|
|**Endoparasite**|Lives _inside_ the host body|Plasmodium (malaria), tapeworm, Ascaris, Wuchereria|

**Brood parasitism** — a fascinating behavioural type, directly asked in NET:

> The **Koel (cuckoo)** lays its eggs in a **crow's nest**. The crow raises the koel's chick, wasting its own resources. The koel benefits (+); the crow is harmed (–). This is brood parasitism.

**Co-evolution in parasitism** — host evolves resistance; parasite evolves to overcome it. Arms race between host and parasite drives evolution of both.

---

### 5. Commensalism (+/0) — One Benefits, Other Unaffected

One species benefits; the other is **completely unaffected** — neither helped nor harmed.

**Key examples for NET:**

|Example|Who benefits|How|
|---|---|---|
|**Barnacles on whale**|Barnacles|Get free transport to food-rich waters|
|**Orchid on tree branch (epiphyte)**|Orchid|Gets sunlight and support; tree unaffected|
|**Cattle egret + cattle**|Egret|Follows cattle, eats insects disturbed by their movement|
|**Remora fish + shark**|Remora|Attaches to shark, eats leftover food scraps|
|**Sucker fish on turtle**|Sucker fish|Gets transport and food|

**Exam trap:** Cattle egret and cattle is often given as mutualism. It is **commensalism** — the cattle gets nothing from the egret. The egret benefits only.

---

### 6. Amensalism (0/–) — One Unaffected, Other Harmed

One species is completely unaffected; the other is harmed — usually through chemical secretion.

**Two forms:**

**Competition amensalism** — a larger/stronger organism inadvertently harms a smaller one just by its presence (e.g. an elephant tramples plants while walking — plants harmed, elephant unaffected).

**Antibiosis** — one organism produces chemicals that harm another. This is the more important type for exams:

- **Penicillium** (fungus) secretes **penicillin** → kills surrounding bacteria → Penicillium unaffected
- **Black walnut tree** secretes **juglone** from roots → inhibits growth of plants nearby (allelopathy)
- **Allelopathy** — chemical inhibition of one plant by another. Very common NET term.

---

### The Niche Concept — Ties Everything Together

**Ecological Niche** = the complete role of a species in its ecosystem — what it eats, where it lives, when it's active, how it interacts with others. It's the species' "profession" in the ecosystem.

- **Habitat** = where the species lives (its "address")
- **Niche** = what it does there (its "profession")

**Fundamental niche** — the full range of conditions a species _could_ live in if there were no competition. **Realised niche** — the actual, smaller range it _does_ occupy due to competition and other interactions.

> A bird _could_ eat from the entire tree (fundamental niche) but competition restricts it to the top branches (realised niche).

**Exam tip:** Realised niche is always smaller than or equal to the fundamental niche — never larger.
