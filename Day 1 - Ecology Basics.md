

Let's start from the very beginning. Before any definition, here's a real-world picture:

> You're standing in a forest. There are trees, birds, insects, soil, water, sunlight, and air. Everything is connected — the bird eats the insect, the insect feeds on the plant, the plant grows in the soil using sunlight. **This entire system — living things + their non-living surroundings + their interactions — is what ecology studies.**

---

## What is Ecology?

**Ecology** is the scientific study of the relationships between living organisms and their environment.

The word comes from Greek: _oikos_ = home, _logos_ = study. So ecology literally means **"study of our home."**

## Food Chain — The Flow of Energy

Energy flows in one direction only: **Sun → Producers → Consumers → Decomposers**
![[Pasted image 20260524124834.png]]
Each step is called a **Trophic Level.****The 10% Law** (by Lindemann, 1942) — only 10% of energy at one trophic level passes to the next. The rest is lost as heat. This is why food chains rarely exceed 4–5 levels, and why there are always more plants than herbivores than carnivores.

**Exam question type:** "Why are food chains limited to 4–5 trophic levels?" → Answer: because of the 10% law, energy depletes rapidly.

---

## Food Web — The Real World

A food chain is an ideal. In nature, animals eat multiple things. A **food web** is a network of interconnected food chains. It is more realistic and more **stable** — if one species disappears, the ecosystem doesn't collapse because other food pathways exist.

**Exam tip:** More interconnections in a food web = greater stability of the ecosystem. This is tested frequently.

## Ecological Pyramids — 3 Types

These show the relationship between trophic levels. Always drawn with producers at the base.
![[Pasted image 20260524154122.png]] 

**The single most important exam fact here:** The **pyramid of energy is ALWAYS upright** — it can never be inverted. Energy is always lost at each step. The pyramids of numbers and biomass CAN be inverted in special cases.

**Classic inverted examples:**

- Numbers: one oak tree supports thousands of insects (inverted at base)
- Biomass: ocean — tiny phytoplankton (low biomass) support large zooplankton populations

## Biogeochemical Cycles — Nature's Recycling

Unlike energy (which flows and is lost), **matter is recycled** in ecosystems. The major cycles you must know for NET are:

|Cycle|Key points for exam|
|---|---|
|**Carbon cycle**|CO₂ fixed by photosynthesis, released by respiration & decomposition. Fossil fuel burning = extra CO₂ = global warming|
|**Nitrogen cycle**|N₂ fixed by bacteria (_Rhizobium_, _Azotobacter_). Steps: fixation → nitrification → assimilation → denitrification|
|**Oxygen cycle**|Released by photosynthesis, consumed by respiration and decomposition|
|**Water cycle**|Evaporation → condensation → precipitation → runoff → infiltration|
|**Phosphorus cycle**|No gaseous phase — moves through rocks, soil, water only. Slowest cycle|

**Exam tip:** Phosphorus cycle has **no atmospheric/gaseous phase** — this is a very common MCQ.

## Types of Ecosystems — Quick Overview

|Type|Examples|Key feature|
|---|---|---|
|Forest|Tropical, temperate, boreal|Highest biodiversity on land|
|Grassland|Savanna, prairie, steppe|Dominated by grasses, seasonal rain|
|Desert|Hot (Sahara) / Cold (Ladakh)|Low rainfall (<25 cm/year)|
|Aquatic — Freshwater|Rivers, lakes, ponds, wetlands|Low salinity|
|Aquatic — Marine|Ocean, coral reefs, estuaries|Covers 70% of Earth, highest biomass|
|Wetlands|Mangroves, swamps, marshes|"Kidneys of Earth" — highest productivity|

**Memory tip for wetlands:** Wetlands = "Kidneys of the Earth." Forests = "Lungs of the Earth." Amazon = "Lungs of the Planet." These appear in MCQs constantly.
